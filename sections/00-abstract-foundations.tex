Three independent traditions --- quantum physics, Whitehead's process
philosophy, and the \bahai writings of \Bahaullah and \AbdulBaha --- converge
on the same structural description of reality: existence is constituted by
relationship, properties emerge only through relational interaction, and the
force sustaining those relationships is, in each tradition's vocabulary,
love.

This paper makes three arguments. First, local substance ontology is
\emph{formally incompatible} with quantum mechanics: Bell's theorem and its
experimental confirmation~\citep{Bell1964,Aspect1982,Hensen2015} undermine
the assumption that systems carry intrinsic properties prior to measurement,
making Whitehead's process ontology a systematically developed alternative
framework for quantum reality. Second, the \bahai writings constitute an
\emph{independent prior instantiation} of process ontology, articulating its
essential claims decades before \citet{Whitehead1929} and roughly a century
before physics confirmed it experimentally. Third, these findings establish
a \emph{triadic convergence}: three traditions, three methodologies, across
170 years, detecting the same feature of reality.

The convergence is formalized through six structural isomorphisms and the
Relational Coherence Framework (\RCF), developed in a companion physics
paper~\citep{Adams2026Physics}, translating the philosophical claim into
testable quantum-mechanical predictions. Each tradition validates its own
thesis independently; the convergence is confirmation by separate
instruments of the same underlying structure. In each tradition's
vocabulary the relational principle sustaining this structure is named
\emph{love} --- \mahabbat in the \bahai lexicon, the creative advance in
Whitehead, and the nonlocal coherence revealed by entanglement --- and this
convergence on a single substantive identification is itself the central
finding.
